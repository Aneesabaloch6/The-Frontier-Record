Amnesty International has described the recent crackdown on protests in Pakistan-administered Jammu and Kashmir as a serious escalation in human rights concerns, following the designation of the Jammu and Kashmir Joint Awami Action Committee (JKJAAC) as a “proscribed organization” under anti-terror laws.

The organization said the authorities’ actions—including internet and mobile shutdowns, mass arrests, raids on organizational offices, and reported use of lethal force—amount to an alarming deterioration of civil liberties in the region. It noted that JKJAAC is a grassroots movement advocating for economic and political rights.

Amnesty International stated that labeling such a movement as “terrorist” on vague grounds, while simultaneously restricting communication and movement, raises serious concerns about proportionality, legality, and respect for human rights.

It further reported that more than 100 people were arrested in connection with the protests, while clashes between protesters and law enforcement resulted in deaths and injuries on both sides. The organization also highlighted reports of targeted arrests of journalists and restrictions on media coverage.

The statement called for independent investigations into alleged unlawful killings, including the death of activist Shahzeb Habib, and urged authorities to ensure accountability for all violence during the crackdown.

Amnesty International emphasized that restrictions on peaceful assembly and freedom of association ahead of regional elections are incompatible with international human rights standards, and called for the lifting of the ban on JKJAAC.

The organization concluded that continued restrictions and use of force risk further escalation and urged authorities to de-escalate tensions and uphold fundamental rights.
